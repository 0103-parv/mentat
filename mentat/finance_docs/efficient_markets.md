# Efficient Market Hypothesis

The Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH), associated with Eugene Fama, holds that asset
prices reflect available information, so consistently earning risk-adjusted excess
returns (alpha) is very hard. It comes in three forms: weak (prices reflect past prices,
so technical analysis shouldn't work), semi-strong (prices reflect all public
information), and strong (prices reflect even private information).

Markets are not perfectly efficient — anomalies exist — but they are adversarial and
near-efficient: once an edge is known and traded, it tends to decay. This is why a
verified market edge is provisional, not permanent, and why broad index data shows
little exploitable structure. It is also why an honest trading agent measures what has
survived out-of-sample rather than claiming to predict the future.
